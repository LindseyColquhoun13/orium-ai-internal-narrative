02 · How We Learn

How we learn

This isn't hypothetical. It's already happening across Orium.

One thing we've learned over the past year is that the best ideas rarely stay where they started.

We've seen the same pattern play out again and again: someone shares a workflow because it saved them an afternoon, a teammate adapts it for their own work, and someone else improves it. Repeat that enough times and it becomes part of how a team works. Over thirty ideas have come through the AI Spotlight Program alone, and a handful of them are already beginning to transform how our teams operate.

A Real Example

Jason noticed a bottleneck slowing another team down, and built a workflow to fix it, for everyone, not just himself.

Jason Toews, a Staff Software Developer, noticed that another team's work had become a bottleneck, not because people weren't capable, but because the technical context they needed for every ticket was scattered across Jira, Confluence, PRDs, pull requests, and meeting notes. Keeping all of that in sync by hand wasn't sustainable. Instead of treating that as someone else's problem, Jason built a workflow that pulls that context together automatically, flags what's missing or out of date, and tags the right person when something still needs an answer.

The result wasn't that Jason got faster. It's that everyone who touched that ticket after him did.
It has been a huge game changer for us... it's really helped with a bottleneck.
· Keddy, Senior Product Manager

None of this happened by accident. Jason set a clear direction, and we've invested across the company to help bring that vision to life. That includes everything from learning experiences and recognition programs to AI-first onboarding, communities of practice, practical tools, and dedicated spaces for people to experiment, learn, and share what works.

We've realized something over the past year. The technology matters, but it isn't the advantage. Our advantage is how quickly good ideas move through Orium.

Our Operating Principle Our real advantage isn't that one person figures something out. It's how quickly everyone else learns from it. That advantage isn't new. AI simply helps good ideas spread faster.
03 · Our POV

How we think about AI

Technology has always changed how work gets done. We believe AI is changing where people create value.

That belief shapes how we've approached AI across Orium.

One thing we're confident about is that the way we work will keep changing. The tools will change. Our workflows will change. Even this guide will change. That's why we're focusing less on teaching one tool and more on building the habits that help us adapt.

You'll start to hear a simple phrase around Orium:

Superhuman Leverage. Human Values.

For us, it means using AI to expand what's possible while making sure people spend more time doing the work that benefits most from their judgement, creativity, and experience.

Part of what makes this hard, and interesting, is that AI is good at working at whatever level you point it at, but it doesn't decide which level actually matters. People are the ones moving between strategy, systems, details, and relationships, deciding where to focus. That's not leftover work. It's the harder, more interesting half of the job, whether you're framing a roadmap, structuring a deal, or debugging a system.

Nik, our VP of Tech, has his own way of explaining this from a development lens: a puzzle analogy that captures where AI excels, and where people still bring something essential. Think of the work as a puzzle. AI is great at finding and placing pieces quickly. What it still can't do is decide what the picture should be, or know which puzzle is even worth solving in the first place.

04 · What Success Looks Like

What success looks like

We aren't adopting AI because we want people doing more work. We're adopting it because we want more of the work you do to actually require you.

Imagine getting to the end of the week and realizing you spent most of it on work that actually required you. Not the repetitive tasks. Not the coordination overhead. Not the first draft AI could've written for you.

You spent it solving real problems, making calls, working with your team, using the judgement nobody else in the room had. That's the week we're building toward.

A quote from an Orian about where AI reduced the repetitive work and freed up time for work with higher impact.

We're already seeing examples of this across Orium. People are getting unstuck in minutes instead of hours. They're spending less time on a slow first draft and more time improving the idea. They're taking on problems they might have avoided six months ago, because AI helps them get started.

What this might feel like

Getting there takes time. Some days it'll feel exciting. Some days it'll feel frustrating. That's part of learning. The direction is the point: less time defending your time, more time spending it on things that matter.

  • You'll probably feel slower than you used to.
  • You'll try things that don't work.
  • Some tools will disappoint you.
  • You'll wonder if everyone else is figuring it out faster than you are.
  • You'll spend less time staring at blank pages.
  • You'll tackle work you used to put off.
  • You'll start noticing opportunities you didn't see before.
  • You'll wonder how you ever worked without some of these habits.

Both of those are normal. Learning almost always feels slower before it feels faster.

Why it's worth it

If we get this right:

  • You'll spend more time on work that benefits from your judgement and experience.
  • You'll learn faster by building on what others discover, instead of figuring everything out yourself.
  • You'll develop habits, like learning quickly, sharing what works, and adapting to change, that will serve you throughout your career.
  • You'll help shape how we work, not just adapt to it.
How We'll Know We're Succeeding We don't think success is measured by how often people use AI tools. We measure it by whether work actually feels different: are people spending more time on work that requires them, are good ideas spreading faster, are we creating more space for judgement, creativity, and collaboration. Those are the signals we're actually paying attention to.

One question we've found helpful along the way:

Is this work benefiting from my experience and judgement, or is it simply consuming my time?

That's worth asking whether AI could help with. Time spent isn't automatically value created.
That's probably where you want to spend more of your energy, not less.

It's not a perfect test. But it's a useful place to start.

06 · Keep Learning

Keep learning

Our value has never come from having all the answers. It comes from asking better questions, applying good judgement, and deciding what actually matters when everything is competing for attention.

None of us has this completely figured out.

Our clients don't.

Our partners don't.

We don't either.

What we do have is a company full of people who are willing to learn, experiment, and share what they discover, so no one has to figure this out on their own. No strategy can build this on its own. It gets built through thousands of small decisions we make every day: what we choose to learn, what we choose to share, and how willing we are to help the next person. That's the part all of us own.
